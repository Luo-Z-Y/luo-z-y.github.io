Auteur Approach: Satoshi Kon

The auteur approach holds that a film’s director is its “author” (French, auteur). It originated in France in the 1950s and seeks to identify the person most responsible for the creation of a film, namely the director. This term is often used to differentiate art film directors from Hollywood’s commercial film directors. Auteurs often work with the art cinema, which “addresses its audience as one of knowledgeable cinemagoers who will recognise the characteristic stylistic touches of the author’s oeuvre” (Pam, 2019). In this light, auteurs use film techniques like mise-en-scene, cinematography, editing, and sound as per the need of their unique narrative, which often stresses meaning and value over popularity. According to Andrew Sarris, there are three premises for auteur theory, which are technical competence, personality, and interior meaning (Sarris, 2007). Under this framework, Satoshi Kon is a prime example of an auteur deserving of the title, from his creation of astonishing and ageless pictures.

Kon’s most iconic signature in films is his match cut editing, this technique is often used to match scene transitions. The beginning four minutes of Paprika has five sequences which all occur in a dream. It features therapist Paprika who is in detective Konakawa Toshimi’s dream (like the film Inception, which made its debut 4 years later), and together they are chasing after a suspect among totally different scenarios, and match cuts help to relate them. Initially Konakawa was in a circus, his circus stunt in the air is mapped to swinging among trees in a forest. Then the vines on the tree are changed to a thin wire a man uses to attempt to kill Konakawa, and the setting changes to a train, Paprika uses a briefcase to hit the man on his head to rescue Konakawa and the briefcase changes to a guitar.

The transition of scenes goes on with match cuts. Kon is playful with connecting films of different genres. The sequence pays homage to The Greatest Show on Earth (1952), the Tarzan series, From Russia with Love (1963), and Roman Holiday (1953) (Kerin, 2010). This makes the beginning chaotic and hard to follow, and it may seem unrealistic even for a dream. Yet, Kon did all those purposefully to highlight the surrealism, which seeks to release the unbridled imagination of the subconscious (James, 2000). He wants the audience to connect to the film subconsciously, and inside the film is a space where common logic may fail, and reality is distorted. Through the shifting identities of Konakawa, the audience experiences the power of cinema which could change their perspectives and identities as well. The match cut is a perfect tool to emphasise continuity and connection despite the swift transitions. It also attracts the audience to become actively engaged in the film. The audience find association between pictures and unleash their imagination to interpret Konakawa’s dream. What seems bizarre on screen is perfectly normal for Konakawa in his dream, for we could all be fine with even the strangest thing in our subconscious world. Once the audience immerse in Konakawa’s dream, the distinction between reality and virtuality becomes vague and therefore they could indulge themselves in the temporal escapism.

Kon’s fast paced editing in Paprika also resembles the hasty tempo of life which people have today. Kon likes ellipsis and would sometimes jump past part of a scene, to speed up even more, he uses fewer frames for swift actions as compared to other films. All Kon’s films are around 90 minutes. Kon believes that today, as an individual or part of the society, we are constantly sensing the world at a faster rate (Every Frame a Painting, 2014). Sometimes we are simply overwhelmed by how much information we possess but could not process. In Paprika, when doctor Toratarō Shima falls victim to the research prototype device DC Mini which can alter people’s dream and goes on a nonsensical tirade, he jumps through a window. Instead of showing how Toratarō falls, Kon stops here and later shows that he is hospitalised. In between the two scenes, we are dragged into Shima’s invaded mind where refrigerator, hydrant, the Statue of Liberty, and many other different objects are marching together with music which the audience could not tell whether diegetic or not as different toys are playing symphony at the same time.

Such a crazy dream is the perfect manifestation of the chaos felt by Shima when his brain is intruded by DC Mini, and the audience could relate. In fact, Kon wants the audience to feel a sense of absurdity and lost not only in the cinema world but also in their real world. Kon likes to use the modern Japanese world as the place of his stories. Establishing shots are given to the central business regions, where sign boards and neon light occupy the whole screen ostentatiously. This is in line with the future digital dystopia he has envisioned for Paprika, a world where the relationship between people becomes more alienated thanks to technology. The interweavement of cold tone of the concrete jungle in real world and the bright colour in the dream makes his film visually impactful, and it further underscores the common issue people today are facing, as they are disappointed by their life, and they can only find consolation in other worlds like in the dream or in the film. To Kon, there is not much difference between the film and dream. A dream is merely a film in our minds. The detective Konakawa in fact used to have a dream of being a director, which he shared with his best friend. This is why Paprika finds him in a theatre in his dream, and he explains film techniques like the classic 180-degree rule.

However, this becomes his lifelong regret as he eventually gave it up. The final scene of his last film, in which the hero shoots the villain, occurs recursively in his dream, and it bites his in the foot and infiltrates his memory when in reality he was chasing after the suspect along the hotel corridor. Kon wants to construct an interesting relationship among the film, dream and reality. What does Konakawa’s dream mean to Paprika? Who has been consciously involved and is aware of the fact she is in a dream. Kon has the left the audience at the same position as Paprika, together we are all in another world, where we have certain superpowers (Paprika becomes Wukong and starts flying, and we audience withhold the power of observing without being noticed), yet we are interacting with the elements inside the other world, through which we are subjected to manipulation emotionally and physically.

Kon has negative perception of technology in his films due to the concern over abusive usage, this renders his films pessimistic outlook. In Perfect Blue, the protagonist Mima Kirigoe was a former pop singer who decides to pursue an acting career. However, her transition into the acting world proves to be a difficult and traumatic experience, as she struggles to cope with the pressures of her new profession and the mysterious stalker who seems to be obsessed with her. In the film, technology plays a role in fragmentise and reduce her to become mere images for different people. The camera, internet, and television screen are used to amplify certain personalities of her while ignoring her as a person, as if she is a toy for others to play with. However, I argue that Kon films are really all about good endings. Across all Kon’s films, he is almost always focusing on the struggle of young females, and how they overcome the difficulties. Be it the mischievous Chiyoko Fujiwara in Millennium Actress, the sweetheart idol Mima Kirigoe in Perfect Blue or even the motherly figure Hana in Tokyo Godfathers. They are all portrayed as the positive, brave, and almost angelic figure. Although in the Japanese anime industry, many works have female leading characters for the sake of attracting male audience, females are sexualised and they are often written not as characters by rather as objects. It is not the case for Kon’s films. His films are never about women relying on men. Girls in Kon’s films do not need male saviours, they are the master of their own destiny. Despite the harsh reality, Mima eventually succeeded in her career. Of course, the audience should take Kon’s warning regarding toxic celebrity culture, and how technology could be misused for cyber stalkers. In Paprika, Doctor Atsuko Chiba is the protagonist with a dual identity. The two sides of her have drastically different personalities. Being a research scientist, she is calm and quiet, but as Paprika in the dream world, she becomes adventurous and proactive. Paprika resembles more to Kon’s general depiction of all the protagonists in his films. The existence of Paprika also challenges people’s perception of dream, as they see it as something stimulated by the actual world. Paprika lives as an individual and she is not under the control of Chiba, she even makes appearances in Chiba’s real life. She is neither a reflection nor a puppet, even though she is fantasised by Chiba.

When being asked about what message he wanted to convey in Perfect Blue during an interview, Kon said, “The real-life images and virtual images come and go quickly in the film. When you are watching the film, you sometimes feel like losing yourself in whichever world you are watching, real or virtual. But after going back and forth between the real world and the virtual world, you eventually find your own identity through your own powers. Nobody can help you do this. You are ultimately the only person who can truly find a place where you know you belong. (Kon, 1997)” This has not changed in the film Paprika, in which Doctor Chiba eventually realises that she should not dodge reality anymore. This is obvious at the end of the film when Paprika leaves Chiba, as Chiba no longer needs Paprika in her world. Paprika used to be the one who is braver and more confident, but Chiba has grown and she now could embrace the reality. Detective Konakawa also reconciled with himself and recognised that being a police officer is no different to his childhood dream of acting as a man who stands by the side of justice in a film. Most of Kon’s films are convoluted and confusing, so it might be hard to find the essence of his films. But I think Millennium Actress has provided a straight-forward answer. The protagonist Chiyoko Fujiwara has been looking for a man she loves for her whole life, but she is doomed to fail as we audience know that the man died long time ago. Kon does not think Fujiwara’s searching as futile. To him, the journey of pursuing something itself is meaningful. And more importantly, the determination and a clear goal help to craft one’s identity. Kon’s films serves as a metacommentary to film itself, he confuses the audience with different layers of dreams and the merge between dream and reality in Paprika. He displays the manipulative power of films to its extreme. But what matters is that we stay afloat of the torrents laced by dream and reality. Escapism is not the ultimate solution, the only way to fight back to the absurdity of life to not succumb to it.

Kon died of pancreatic cancer at the age of 46 in 2010 and left behind an unfinished potential masterpiece Dreaming Machine. However, he has already started the invention of Dream Machine long time ago, and the objective of such a creation is clear: By underscoring both the beauty and danger of the dream, he wants people to fulfil themselves in their real life. Perhaps it is destined for a film titled Dreaming Machine to not make its way to the real world.

Farewell, Satoshi Kon.
